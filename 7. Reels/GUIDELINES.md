# Reel Script Guidelines — Project 100

## What these reels are for

Drive people to project100.dev to submit their problem. That's the primary job.
Secondary: build an audience around the challenge itself — people who want to follow
the journey even if they don't have a problem to submit right now.

---

## Your voice

- Talk like you're explaining something to a friend, not presenting to a room
- Be honest about where you are. You're learning. That's the story, not a weakness
- Short sentences. If you wouldn't say it out loud, cut it
- No em dashes. No buzzwords (leverage, ecosystem, game-changer, unlock)
- Don't oversell AI. It's the tool, not the point. The problem is the point
- First person always. "I built", "I helped", "I'm doing this because"

---

## Two audiences — write for one at a time

**Audience A — The problem-haver**
Small business owner, freelancer, someone doing admin they hate, a person with a
process that's broken. They're not necessarily tech-savvy. They don't care about AI.
They care about getting their thing fixed.

Speak to their frustration first. Then offer the solution. Make it feel relevant to them.

**Audience B — The curious follower**
Interested in AI, building, the challenge itself. They want to watch the journey.
They might share it, they might submit a problem eventually, they might just follow along.

Speak to the "wait, he's doing WHAT for free?" angle. The challenge is interesting to them.

---

## What to steal from your reference creators

**alex.petrakieva** — The personal journey framing. She makes her own situation the
entry point into the topic. Don't start with "here's what AI can do." Start with
something real that happened to you or someone you helped.

**higherupwellness** — The direct cut. No long intro, no "hey guys." He starts mid-thought.
Steal the confrontational opener: "Most businesses are wasting hours on something they
could fix in a day." Makes people stop scrolling.

**jun_yuh** — Founder POV storytelling. He talks about what he's building and why.
Use this for the behind-the-scenes style reels: what you're working on, what the challenge
looks like from the inside, lessons from a project.

**jadenlevin** — The CTA is built into the content, not added at the end. By the time
he says "comment below" the viewer already wants to. Work the ask into the story.

**lostandlucky** — Community feel. Talks to people, not at them. The viewer feels like
they're in on something, not being sold to.

## Script structures — pick one per reel

### Structure 1 — The Hook/Story/Ask (best for Audience A)
Use when: you want to get submissions. Problem-focused.

```
HOOK      — A line that stops the scroll. One sentence. Could be a stat, a question,
            a bold statement, or a situation they recognise.

STORY     — What happened, what you built, what problem it solved. Keep it specific.
            Vague doesn't connect. "A restaurant owner was..." beats "businesses often..."

ASK       — Soft and direct. Not "CLICK THE LINK IN MY BIO!!!" Just: "If that sounds
            like you, the link's in my bio. Tell me your problem."
```

### Structure 2 — The Challenge Update (best for Audience B)
Use when: you want to grow your audience, share progress, build the journey narrative.

```
STATE     — Where you are. "I'm on project 4 of 100."

SHOW      — What you just built or learned. Specific detail makes it credible.

REFLECT   — One honest thought. Could be what surprised you, what was harder than
            expected, what you'd do differently.

HOOK FORWARD — Leave them wanting to see what's next.
```

### Structure 3 — The Myth-Bust (best for either audience)
Use when: you want to challenge a common belief about AI or about "needing tech skills."

```
STATEMENT — A belief people hold. State it plainly.
FLIP      — Why that's not quite right, from your experience.
PROOF     — One specific example that backs it up.
CLOSE     — What that means for the viewer.
```

---

## CTAs — keep them soft

Good:
- "If you have a problem worth solving, the link's in my bio."
- "Tell me your problem in the comments."
- "Project 100. Link in bio."
- "I'm looking for the next one. Send me your thing."

Avoid:
- "CLICK THE LINK IN MY BIO RIGHT NOW"
- "Don't miss out"
- Anything with urgency that sounds manufactured

---

## Things to avoid

- Starting with "Hey guys" or "So today I wanted to talk about..."
- Explaining what AI is before getting to the point
- Making it sound bigger than it is ("I'm CHANGING the way businesses work")
- Long pauses, filler words in the script (ums and ahs are fine when filming, just
  don't write them in)
- Ending on a weak close ("Anyway, yeah, that's about it")

---

## Quick checklist before you film

- [ ] Does the first line make someone stop scrolling?
- [ ] Is there one specific, concrete detail in the middle? (a name, a number, a situation)
- [ ] Does it feel like you're talking to one person, not an audience?
- [ ] Is the ask clear without being pushy?
- [ ] Would you watch this if you saw it on your feed?
